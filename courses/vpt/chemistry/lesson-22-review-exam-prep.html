Gen Chem I — CHM 111 (Weeks 1–11)
Week 1–3: Atomic Structure & Periodic Trends

The atomic model (Bohr → quantum mechanical) establishes electron configuration, which drives ALL periodic trends: atomic radius, ionization energy, electronegativity, and electron affinity. These trends determine how atoms bond and react.

Connects to: Bonding (ionic/covalent character) → VSEPR & Polarity → Intermolecular Forces
Week 4–5: Bonding, VSEPR & Molecular Geometry

Lewis structures reveal bonding patterns and formal charges. VSEPR uses electron geometry to predict molecular shape, which determines polarity. Polarity drives intermolecular forces (dispersion, dipole-dipole, H-bonding), which control physical properties like bp and solubility.

Connects to: Thermochemistry (bond enthalpy) → Solutions (like dissolves like)
Week 6–7: Stoichiometry & Solution Chemistry

The mole is the universal currency. Balanced equations give mole ratios; limiting reagent determines theoretical yield. Molarity links volume and moles in solution, enabling titration calculations. Every quantitative problem in chemistry ultimately reduces to a mole ratio conversion.

Connects to: All quantitative problems (equilibrium, electro, nuclear)
Week 8–9: Gas Laws

Ideal Gas Law (PV=nRT) unifies all gas law relationships. Real gases deviate at high pressure and low temperature — van der Waals corrects for this. Kinetic Molecular Theory connects gas behavior to molecular motion; Graham's Law follows from KMT. Gas stoichiometry combines mole ratios with PV=nRT.

Connects to: Thermodynamics (entropy of gases) → Equilibrium (Kp)
Week 10–11: Thermochemistry

Enthalpy (ΔH) measures heat at constant pressure. Hess's Law lets you build reactions from pieces — it's additive because H is a state function. Formation enthalpies, bond enthalpies, and calorimetry all calculate ΔH by different routes. This sets up the full thermodynamic picture (ΔG = ΔH − TΔS) in Gen Chem II.

Connects to: ΔG & spontaneity → Equilibrium constant K
Gen Chem II — CHM 112 (Weeks 12–21)
Week 12–13: Chemical Equilibrium

Equilibrium is reached when forward and reverse rates are equal. K expresses the ratio of products to reactants at equilibrium. ICE tables are the systematic tool. Le Chatelier's Principle predicts how K responds to stress (concentration, temperature, pressure). K is temperature-dependent; K and ΔG° are linked by ΔG° = −RT ln K.

Connects to: Ksp (solubility) → Ka/Kb (acid-base) → ΔG (thermo)
Week 14–15: Acids, Bases & Buffers

Strong acids/bases fully dissociate; weak ones establish equilibrium (Ka, Kb). pH and pOH are logarithmic scales. Neutralization reactions produce salts whose hydrolysis shifts pH. Buffers (Henderson-Hasselbalch) resist pH change — critical in biology and industry. Titration curves reveal equivalence points and pKa values directly.

Connects to: Solubility equilibria (common ion) → Biochemical applications
Week 16–17: Chemical Kinetics

Kinetics answers how fast a reaction goes. Rate laws are empirical (from data), not from stoichiometry. Integrated rate laws determine order; half-life depends on order. Arrhenius connects rate to temperature via activation energy. Catalysts lower Ea without changing K or ΔG — they affect kinetics, not thermodynamics.

Connects to: Equilibrium (forward/reverse rates) → Nuclear decay (1st order)
Week 18–19: Thermodynamics (Full)

Extends thermochemistry: entropy (ΔS) measures disorder — gases have highest entropy. ΔG = ΔH − TΔS determines spontaneity. When ΔG° = 0, K = 1. ΔG° = −nFE° links all three: thermodynamics, electrochemistry, and equilibrium into one equation. This is the heart of physical chemistry.

Connects to: Electrochemistry (E° and ΔG) → All K expressions
Week 20: Electrochemistry

Redox reactions drive electrochemical cells. Galvanic cells produce work (E°_cell > 0); electrolytic cells consume it. E°_cell, ΔG°, and K are all connected. The Nernst equation adjusts E for non-standard conditions. Faraday's Law links charge, time, and mass deposited — critical for electrolysis calculations.

Connects to: Corrosion, batteries, Navy hull protection (sacrificial anodes)
Week 21: Nuclear Chemistry

Radioactive decay is a first-order process (same math as kinetics!). Alpha, beta, and gamma decay change nuclear composition. Half-life and decay constants use the same integrated rate law: N = N₀e^(−λt). Nuclear binding energy (from mass defect) and Einstein's E=mc² explain fusion and fission energetics. Applications: nuclear reactors, medical imaging, dating.

Connects to: First-order kinetics (t₁/₂, rate constant)
Electrochemistry is directly relevant to your Navy service: sacrificial zinc anodes protect ship hulls from galvanic corrosion (same principles as galvanic cells). Nuclear reactors aboard carriers use sustained fission chain reactions — the same binding energy concepts from nuclear chemistry.
Mistake 01
Forgetting to Convert Temperature to Kelvin
Using °C in PV=nRT, the Arrhenius equation, ΔG=ΔH−TΔS, or the Nernst equation. At 25°C that's 298 K — off by 273, which destroys your calculation.
Always write T = ___ + 273.15 = ___ K as your first line before plugging into any formula with T.
Mistake 02
Putting Solids/Liquids in K or Q Expressions
Pure solids and pure liquids have activity = 1 and are OMITTED from K, Kp, Ksp, Ka, Kb expressions. Including [H₂O] or [CaCO₃(s)] will give wrong K values.
Before writing K, label each species as (g), (aq), (l), or (s). Only (g) and (aq) go in the expression.
Mistake 03
Sign Errors in Hess's Law
Reversing a reaction changes the sign of ΔH. Multiplying by a coefficient scales ΔH by the same factor. Students often flip reactions without flipping the sign, or forget to scale ΔH when scaling reaction stoichiometry.
For each step: "Did I reverse it? Flip sign. Did I multiply by n? Multiply ΔH by n." Write it out explicitly.
Mistake 04
Using Stoichiometric Coefficients as Reaction Orders
Rate orders must be determined from experimental data. For A + 2B → C, the rate law is NOT automatically rate = k[A][B]². This is true only for elementary steps — overall reactions require data.
Rate orders come from comparing rate experiments. Look for "doubling [A] doubles the rate" → first order in A. Coefficients are irrelevant for overall rate laws.
Mistake 05
Confusing Molarity and Molality in Colligative Properties
ΔTb = Kb·m uses molality (mol solute / kg solvent), not molarity. For dilute aqueous solutions they're close, but on exams they may differ. Osmotic pressure π = MRT uses molarity.
Boiling point, freezing point, vapor pressure → molality (m). Osmotic pressure → molarity (M). Memorize which uses which.
Mistake 06
Cathode/Anode Sign Confusion in Electrochemistry
In a galvanic cell, the anode is negative (oxidation) and cathode is positive (reduction). In electrolytic cells, the signs flip externally but oxidation still occurs at the anode. Students mix up which electrode gets which label.
Memory: "An Ox, Red Cat" — ANode = OXidation, REDuction = CAThode. Works for both galvanic and electrolytic cells.
Mistake 07
Forgetting the ICE Table "Change" Row Direction
Students set up ICE tables with +x for both reactants and products, or put −x on the products side. The change row must decrease reactants and increase products (or reverse, if Q > K).
ICE: Reactants go −(coeff)x; Products go +(coeff)x. Then check: does your x give positive equilibrium concentrations? If x comes out negative, flip direction.
Mistake 08
Wrong R Value for the Problem
R = 0.0821 L·atm/mol·K for gas law problems (pressure in atm). R = 8.314 J/mol·K for thermodynamic problems (energy in joules). Using the wrong R gives answers off by a factor of ~100.
Ask: does your equation output energy (J, kJ)? Use 8.314. Does it involve pressure (atm) and volume (L)? Use 0.0821.
Mistake 09
Sig Fig Errors and Logarithm Rules
For pH = −log[H⁺]: the number of decimal places in pH equals the sig figs of [H⁺]. pH = 3.45 has 2 sig figs in the mantissa. Students either round too early or report too many digits.
Don't round intermediate steps. Carry one extra digit through calculations. Apply sig fig rules only at the final answer. For logs, sig figs → decimal places.
Mistake 10
Misidentifying Limiting Reagent
Students divide grams by molar mass to get moles, then compare moles directly without accounting for stoichiometric coefficients. You must divide each reactant's moles by its stoichiometric coefficient to find the limiting reagent.
Divide each reactant's moles by its balanced-equation coefficient. The smallest result is the limiting reagent. Then use only that reactant's moles for yield calculation.

CHM 111-112 exam-specific tactics drawn from what actually earns points — and what loses them.

1
Read Every Problem Twice Before Starting
Identify: What is given? What are the units? What is being asked? Many points are lost by solving for the wrong quantity. Circle the unknown. Underline the given values. Write the target unit before plugging in numbers.
2
Dimensional Analysis for Every Calculation
Write units with every number and cancel them explicitly. If the units do not work out to the target unit, you have the wrong formula or wrong conversion. This catches ~80% of setup errors before you finish the problem. Non-negotiable habit.
3
ICE Tables — Always Draw Them
For any equilibrium, Ka, Kb, or buffer problem, immediately set up an ICE table even if you think you can do it in your head. Write I, C, E rows. Fill in known values. Use +x / −x for the change row. This structure prevents sign errors and keeps partial credit visible to graders.
4
Significant Figures — Apply at the End Only
Carry at least one extra digit through intermediate steps. Only round to correct sig figs at the final answer. For addition/subtraction: sig figs by decimal places. For multiplication/division: sig figs by count. For logs (pH): sig figs → decimal places of result.
5
Multi-Part Problems — Work Forward Even If Part A Is Wrong
If you cannot solve part A, write a reasonable assumed value and use it for parts B and C. Most graders give full credit for parts B and C if your method is correct, regardless of part A. Label it "Assume [A] = X from part A." Partial credit is real — never leave a part blank.
6
Redox and Electrochemistry — Balance Half-Reactions First
For any redox problem: separate into oxidation and reduction half-reactions. Balance atoms (use H₂O for O, H⁺ for H in acidic; OH⁻ in basic). Balance charge with electrons. Combine by multiplying to cancel electrons. Check: total charge must balance. Then apply E° = cathode − anode.
7
Choosing the Correct R — Two-Second Check
Look at the output units of your equation. Energy output (J, kJ, V, ΔG) → use R = 8.314 J/mol·K. Pressure-volume output (atm, L) → use R = 0.0821 L·atm/mol·K. Write R = ___ on your scratch paper before computing. This single check prevents one of the most common calculation errors.
8
Thermodynamics Sign Convention
ΔH < 0 = exothermic (heat released to surroundings). ΔH > 0 = endothermic (heat absorbed). ΔS > 0 = more disorder (gases formed, particles increase). ΔG < 0 = spontaneous. E°_cell > 0 = spontaneous (galvanic). All signs must be internally consistent. If your ΔG is negative but your E° is also negative, recheck.
9
Time Management — 2 Minutes Per Point
For a 100-point, 2-hour exam: allocate ~1.2 min/point. Skip problems that are taking >3× expected time — mark them and return. Do concept/recognition questions first (MC, true/false). Then short calculations. Save multi-part derivations for last. This ensures you collect every easy point before getting bogged down.
10
Show All Work — Even for "Simple" Steps
Write the formula, substitute values with units, then compute. Even if the final answer is wrong, graders award method points for correct setup. A wrong answer with no work shown gets 0. A wrong answer with correct setup might get 60-80% of the points. This is the highest-ROI exam habit in any chemistry course.
