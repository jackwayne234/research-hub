Types of Intermolecular Forces
▼

Intermolecular forces (IMFs) are electrostatic attractions between molecules — distinct from the intramolecular covalent or ionic bonds within molecules. They govern bulk physical properties: boiling point, viscosity, surface tension, vapor pressure.

Hierarchy of Strength
Ion–Ion > Ion–Dipole > H-bond > Dipole–Dipole > London Dispersion
Force Strength (kJ/mol) Who Has It Origin
Ion–Ion 400–4000 Ionic crystals (NaCl, MgO) Coulombic: q+q−/r
Ion–Dipole 40–600 Ions dissolved in polar solvents Ion charge + permanent dipole
Hydrogen Bond 10–40 N–H, O–H, F–H donors with N/O/F acceptors Partial covalence + electrostatics
Dipole–Dipole 3–20 All polar molecules Permanent electric dipoles
London Dispersion 0.05–40 ALL molecules and atoms Instantaneous quantum fluctuations
Key Rule: London dispersion is universal — every molecule has it. For nonpolar molecules, it is the only IMF. For large nonpolar molecules (e.g., I2, long alkane chains), London forces can exceed dipole-dipole forces of smaller polar molecules.
Navy Context
When seawater contacts metal, ion–dipole forces drive hydration of Na+ and Cl−, accelerating corrosion. Understanding which ions hydrate most strongly guides materials selection for salt-water environments. Mg2+ (higher charge density) hydrates more strongly than Na+ — relevant to seawater chemistry in nuclear and conventional propulsion.
02
London Dispersion Forces
▼

London dispersion forces (LDF) arise from temporary dipoles created by momentary asymmetry in an electron cloud. This asymmetry induces a complementary dipole in a neighboring molecule, creating a net attractive force.

Quantum Mechanics Connection
LDFs are a direct consequence of quantum zero-point motion. Electrons are not stationary — the Heisenberg uncertainty principle requires non-zero kinetic energy even at absolute zero. This continuous quantum fluctuation of electron density creates transient dipole moments. London (1930) derived the interaction energy using second-order perturbation theory:

ULDF ≈ −(3/2) × (α₁α₂/r⁶) × (I₁I₂)/(I₁+I₂)

where α is polarizability and I is ionization energy. The r−6 dependence is identical to the attractive term in the Lennard-Jones potential.

Factors Increasing LDF Strength

↑ M
Higher molar mass = more electrons = more polarizable
↑ SA
Greater surface area = more contact points = stronger total force
Branching Effect

Linear chain > branched chain for LDF. n-pentane (bp 36°C) > neopentane (bp 9.5°C) — same formula C5H12, but neopentane is nearly spherical, minimizing surface contact.

Navy Context — JP-5 Fuel
JP-5 jet fuel is a mixture of C8–C16 hydrocarbons (nonpolar). Its vapor pressure and volatility are entirely controlled by London dispersion forces. Higher molar mass cuts in the blend have stronger LDFs → lower vapor pressure → less flammability at ambient temps. The Navy mandates JP-5 over JP-4 aboard carriers precisely because its higher flash point (60°C vs −23°C) reduces fire risk in enclosed spaces.
03
Dipole–Dipole Interactions
▼

Polar molecules have a permanent electric dipole moment (μ) due to unequal electronegativity between bonded atoms. These permanent dipoles align head-to-tail, with partial positive (δ+) ends attracted to partial negative (δ−) ends of neighboring molecules.

Dipole-Dipole Energy (orientationally averaged)
UD-D ≈ −(2μ₁²μ₂²) / (3kT r⁶)

Temperature dependent (T in denominator): at higher T, thermal motion disrupts alignment, weakening the effective interaction. London forces have no such T dependence in the same form.

Electrostatics Connection

A dipole is simply two charges separated by a distance d. The potential energy between two aligned dipoles falls off as r−3 (for fixed alignment) or r−6 (thermally averaged). This is classical electrostatics — Coulomb's law applied to charge distributions.

Molar Mass vs. Polarity: HBr (bp −67°C) boils higher than HCl (bp −85°C) despite having a smaller dipole, because HBr has greater molar mass (80.9 vs 36.5 g/mol), giving it stronger London forces that outweigh the dipole-dipole advantage of HCl.
Must-Know: When to Compare IMF Types

For molecules of similar molar mass, the more polar one boils higher. For molecules of very different molar masses, London forces can dominate even if one is nonpolar. Always consider both LDF and dipole-dipole together.

04
Hydrogen Bonding
▼

Hydrogen bonding is a special case of dipole-dipole interaction — but much stronger because hydrogen bonded to a highly electronegative atom (N, O, F) becomes nearly a bare proton. The resulting positive electrostatic potential interacts strongly with the lone pairs of N, O, or F on adjacent molecules.

Partial Covalence — QM Connection
Modern quantum chemical analysis (symmetry-adapted perturbation theory, SAPT) shows the hydrogen bond is not purely electrostatic. At close range (~1.8–2.0 Å), there is measurable orbital overlap between the lone pair of the acceptor and the σ* antibonding orbital of the X–H donor. This charge transfer (partial covalence) accounts for ~10–20% of the total H-bond energy in water and explains why H-bonds are directional (linear geometry preferred) unlike purely electrostatic interactions.
H-bond Requirements
Donor: N–H, O–H, or F–H   |   Acceptor: lone pair on N, O, or F

Without H-bonding, water's predicted bp would be ~−80°C (extrapolating from H2S, H2Se, H2Te). The actual +100°C represents a 180°C anomaly driven entirely by H-bonds.

Each water molecule can form up to 4 hydrogen bonds (2 donor O–H bonds, 2 acceptor lone pairs). This tetrahedral H-bond network gives water its extraordinary properties.

Water Anomalies Explained by H-bonds

Density Maximum at 4°C

Ice has a rigid, open H-bond lattice — less dense than liquid water. As ice melts (0–4°C), H-bonds partially break, allowing molecules to pack more closely (density increases). Above 4°C, thermal expansion wins (density decreases). Ice floats → ice–water interface → aquatic life survives under ice cover.

High Heat Capacity

cp(H₂O) = 4.184 J/(g·K) — highest of common liquids. Energy goes into breaking/bending H-bonds before raising temperature. This thermal buffering capacity is a direct result of the extensive H-bond network and is irreplaceable in engineering applications.

DNA Base Pairing

H-bonds are the selective force behind DNA's double helix. Adenine–Thymine (A–T): 2 H-bonds. Guanine–Cytosine (G–C): 3 H-bonds. The difference in H-bond count gives G–C pairs greater thermodynamic stability, explaining why GC-rich DNA sequences have higher melting temperatures.

Navy Context — Water as Coolant
Naval reactors use water as both moderator and coolant. Water's exceptional specific heat (4.184 J/g·K), vaporization enthalpy (2257 J/g at 100°C), and liquid range under pressure (at 15 MPa, bp ≈ 342°C) all trace back to hydrogen bonding. No substitute liquid approaches this thermal performance at similar cost and availability. H-bonds also make water an excellent neutron moderator due to its high hydrogen density.
05
Effects on Physical Properties
▼

Boiling Point

bp is set by the energy required to separate molecules from the liquid phase (overcome IMFs). Stronger IMFs → higher bp. Add all active IMFs; the total determines the trend.

Clausius-Clapeyron (connecting bp to IMFs)
ln(P₂/P₁) = −ΔHvap/R × (1/T₂ − 1/T₁)

ΔHvap is the molar enthalpy of vaporization — directly reflects total IMF strength. Stronger IMFs → larger ΔHvap → higher bp (where P = 1 atm).

Vapor Pressure

Vapor pressure is inversely related to IMF strength. Strong IMFs keep molecules in the liquid phase → fewer molecules escape → low vapor pressure. At fixed temperature, more volatile = weaker IMFs.

Vapor Pressure Ranking Examples (at 25°C)
Diethyl ether (540 mmHg) > Ethanol (59 mmHg) > Water (24 mmHg) > Glycerol (0.003 mmHg)

IMF trend (weakest to strongest): ether (LDF+weak D-D) < ethanol (H-bond, 1 OH) < water (H-bond, 2 OH donors) < glycerol (3 OH groups, extensive H-bond network)

Viscosity

Viscosity is resistance to flow — a measure of intermolecular friction. Strong IMFs + molecular entanglement (long chains) → high viscosity. Temperature reduces viscosity in liquids (thermal energy overcomes IMFs).

Surface Tension

Molecules at a liquid's surface have fewer neighbors, making them energetically unfavorable. Strong IMFs → high surface tension as the liquid minimizes surface area. Water's surface tension (72 mN/m at 20°C) is very high due to H-bonding.

Capillary Action

Results from competition between adhesion (liquid–surface IMFs) and cohesion (liquid–liquid IMFs). Water rises in glass because adhesion to SiO2 (via H-bonding with silanol groups) exceeds cohesion. Mercury falls in glass — cohesion (metallic bonds) exceeds adhesion.

Navy Context — Lubricants and Fuel
Ship lubricating oils (high-viscosity hydrocarbons with polar additives) rely on both LDF (chain length) and dipole-dipole interactions to maintain film strength under pressure. JP-5 fuel's vapor pressure (~2 mmHg at 20°C) is low enough to be safe in enclosed carrier environments. Hydraulic fluids on aircraft carriers are rated by viscosity index — how much viscosity drops with temperature, governed by the temperature sensitivity of their IMFs.

Types of Solids & Metallic Bonding
▼

The type of solid is determined by the particles at lattice points and the interactions between them. This dictates all physical properties.

Solid TypeParticlesForcesmp (°C)ConductivityExamples
Molecular Neutral molecules IMFs (LDF, D-D, H-bond) −270 to ~300 None (insulator) Ice, sugar, dry ice, iodine
Ionic Cations + anions Ion–ion (Coulombic) 600–3000 None (solid); yes (molten/dissolved) NaCl, CaF2, MgO
Metallic Cations in e⁻ sea Metallic bonding (band theory) −39 (Hg) to 3422 (W) Excellent (free e⁻) Fe, Cu, Al, W
Network Covalent Atoms Covalent bonds throughout 1000–4000 None (diamond); yes (graphite, Si) Diamond, SiO2, SiC, graphite

Network Covalent Solids

In diamond, every carbon is sp3 hybridized with 4 covalent bonds extending in 3D. There are no discrete molecules — the entire crystal is one giant covalent molecule. This requires breaking covalent bonds to melt (~3550°C), explaining the extreme hardness and melting point.

SiO₂ (Quartz) — 3D Covalent Network

Each Si bonds to 4 O atoms; each O bridges two Si atoms. Formula is SiO2 but structure is a continuous 3D lattice. mp = 1650°C. Used in fiber optics, semiconductors, and naval radar domes.

Metallic Bonding — Band Theory

In a metal, atomic orbitals from N atoms combine into N molecular orbitals, forming a near-continuous energy band. In conductors, the valence band is partially filled (or overlaps the conduction band), allowing electrons to move freely under an applied field.

Band Theory Connection to QM
Free electrons in a metal are described by the Fermi-Dirac distribution. At T = 0 K, all states below the Fermi level (EF) are occupied; all above are empty. At finite T, electrons near EF are thermally promoted. The density of states at EF determines electrical and thermal conductivity. This explains why metals conduct heat and electricity by the same mechanism — electron transport.
Wiedemann-Franz Law (band theory result)
κ / σ = L × T

Thermal conductivity κ divided by electrical conductivity σ equals the Lorenz number L times temperature. Good electrical conductors are also good thermal conductors — because the same free electrons carry both. Used in heat sink design for electronic systems aboard ships.

Navy Context — Materials Selection
Hull steel (BCC metallic solid) vs. aluminum superstructure (FCC metallic) vs. fiberglass (amorphous network) vs. Lexan (molecular solid polymer). Each material's mechanical and thermal properties trace to its solid type. Network SiC ceramics are used in ship armor inserts; their hardness comes from the 3D covalent network. Metallic copper alloys in propeller shafts leverage free-electron conductivity and corrosion resistance.

Problem 3 — Vapor Pressure

At 25°C, which compound has the highest vapor pressure?

Ether has no O–H, so no H-bond donation (O is acceptor only, and no H-bond donor partner here). Weakest IMF → highest vapor pressure (~540 mmHg). Glycerol (3 OH groups) has the lowest (~0.003 mmHg).

Problem 4 — Solid Type

A crystalline solid melts at 2072°C, does not conduct electricity in any state, and is extremely hard. What type of solid is it?

Ionic conducts when melted. Metallic always conducts. Molecular solids have low mp. Network covalent: no free ions or electrons, 3D covalent bonds → non-conducting, extreme mp, extremely hard. (MgO at 2072°C is actually ionic — a tricky case; diamond at 3550°C and SiC at ~2730°C are network covalent non-conductors.)
